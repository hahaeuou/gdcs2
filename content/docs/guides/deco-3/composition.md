Evenness is one of the two biggest design concepts, along with Hierarchy. To explain it shortly, evenness is dividing a design element into similar parts. This guide will explain its importance for design and how you can improve your work by being aware of it.

# 1: What Is Evenness?

In simple terms, evenness is when a **decoration element lacks contrast**. If you have two lines with the same length, they're even. The same goes if you have two shapes with the same size, amount of detail, and so on.

{{< img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1YJlAUq32ohVTg70W9Y-IXCFl5uH8LKJz" >}}

This matters because **evenness makes your deco less dynamic**. It's fine to have some evenness as you'll see later, but **too much of it makes your deco feel boring and unnatural**. For instance, if you look at the real world (yes, that means going outside), objects in nature rarely have evenness. Making your deco more dynamic helps guide the eye around, which makes things more interesting. As such, that means avoiding evenness.

Additionally, **evenness makes it hard to "read" your deco**. With the squares in the image above, it's hard to tell which square is more important on the left side. On the right side, since one of them is clearly larger than the other, it's easy to see which one is more important.

{{< callout context="caution" title="" icon="outline/alert-triangle" >}}
Basically, **avoid perfectly dividing things in two**, and **make sure you have clear contrast**.
{{< /callout >}}

While it's effectively impossible to remove *all* evenness from your deco, reducing it as much as possible will greatly help your design skills.

## Where Evenness Occurs

Evenness can occur with any design element. Here are some examples of it occurring.

- **Lines:** Lines with the same length are even. This also applies to the lines that make up shapes, such as in the image above.
{{< img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1TlmfJ56lPJNgQl52XtjITs1YQopgkQVo">}}

- **Curves:** Curves with clear symmetry are typically even. This is a clear example of evenness making deco less dynamic, as the curves on the right side direct the eye much more effectively.
{{< img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1BoPOTNK5FaWWgld5RVMpICX_qpkXpUVn">}}

- **Angles:** Shapes with the same interior angles inside them are even. This occurs frequently for good reason in manmade designs (e.g., squares & rectangles) but is much harder to find in nature.
{{< img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1WYEc-dD7m5A1ZcNOv7XGEkrD9A_sG2cu">}}

- **Size:** Shapes with the same size are even. The example above shows this pretty clearly. However, this can also apply to shapes that look completely different but take up the same area.

- **Color:** Colors that take up the same *total area on screen* are even. This is similar to the size example, but the colors don't have to be directly next to each other for this to happen.
  - This also applies to components of color, such as value and saturation.
{{< img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1gk7D9EAe0kOU53Fmergrt6UrIp4pqmw8">}}

- **Detail:** Objects with the same level of detail are even. This also applies detailing methods such as rendering and texturing.

## How Evenness Occurs

There are many common ways for evenness to unintentionally sneak itself into your levels. The image below has a few examples of it; try to find them for yourself before moving on.

### Issue 1: Sandwiching

This occurs when you have **many even details in one area**.

Usually, sandwiching is a result of bad detail grouping, meaning your details are evenly spread out. This is why the [Making Blocks](/docs/guides/deco-1/making-blocks/#3-making-details) guide recommends placing most details at the *edges* and *corners* of blocks; it helps group details better and avoid sandwiching.

Sandwiching is very noticeable on the wrecked car model in the image above. The red lines represent areas with the same length, while the purple areas have similar sizes.

{{< img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1P8Rbr2dytzsUkVjohO_3DfdQQtDxlrUp">}}

### Issue 2: Hidden Lengths

It is typically easier to notice evenness when it occurs with horizontal or vertical parts – for instance, shapes that are neatly lined up perfectly. But evenness doesn't always occur in such neat ways. Sometimes, **evenness occurs with diagonals** and in other subtle ways.

This can be especially common with shapes like triangles, and it's particularly hard to remove because of how those shapes work.

The rocks in the image above contain many of these hidden lengths. The shapes where they occur are highlighted in purple, while the actual evenness is marked with the red lines.

{{< img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1-5NUKLj5MUhFIpOiUCIqxyQa-TwIaXOT">}}

### Issue 3: Complexity

Complex deco has far more moving parts, which makes it that much easier to **introduce evenness on accident**.

The examples above are both caused in part by complexity. Had I made the decoration simpler in those examples, the sandwiching and hidden lengths might have been easy to avoid.

# 2: Managing Evenness

This section goes over how to spot evenness in your work, ways to fix it, and some notable exceptions to the theory.

While avoiding evenness is important, it's not the end of the world if you have some in your work. Additionally, the way you try to fix it can lead to more problems down the line if you aren't careful.

As such, it's important to tackle these techniques in order, and learn them *after* you already have the skills to comfortably decorate.

## Spotting Evenness

**The only way to get good with identifying evenness is to constantly see it at work.** Take the images below, save them to your device, and use a software to mark all the evenness you can find. Each example will only get more difficult, so master the simple examples before moving on.

I recommend starting by trying to identify shapes with even lengths or widths. Then, try to identify shapes with even sizes. Afterwards, you can focus on identifying colors with an even total area, and objects with even detailing.

## Fixing Evenness

There are a few ways to fix evenness, each with their own pros and cons.

{{< tabs "evenness-solutions" >}}
{{< tab "Move, Rotate, & Scale" >}}

This solution is the simplest: simply move, rotate, and scale objects to remove evenness.

For instance, in the original image at the start of this guide, I scaled one square up on its Y axis and scaled the other down, which removed the evenness.

**Pros:**
- Simple and easy to do.
- Very effective and versatile; can be used in many situations.

**Cons:**
- Can be very limited in certain situations - for instance, it's hard to move objects around when they affect gameplay.
- May not actually fix the underlying evenness; in the worst scenarios, it's basically useless.

{{< /tab >}}
{{< tab "Simplify" >}}

As mentioned before, evenness can get worse the more complex your deco is. As such, removing details and shapes you don't *really* need can help fix (or simplify) the issue.

In general, it's important to eliminate evenness before you add complexity to your deco. That way, you'll avoid having to do this step.

**Pros:**
- Reduces the problem scope: instead of fixing 50 evenness problems, you only have to deal with 10.

**Cons:**
- Very limited when every detail is essential (gameplay objects, for instance).
- Can lead to other problems if your underlying design is boring: removing details could make your deco look worse.

{{< /tab >}}
{{< tab "Reduce Contrast" >}}

Evenness matters more when it's noticeable, which will be explained in the Hierarchy guide. As such, if you can't remove it entirely, you can simply try to hide it.

For instance, evenness can occur frequently inside textures or static, but it doesn't matter as much because you're not combing through a texture to find every even pixel.

**Pros:**
- Very easy to do.
- Most people won't notice unless they're trained to spot evenness.

**Cons:**
- Most limited option, as removing contrast can cause major problems in your deco. I suggest only doing this on the least important details, and *not* on things like gameplay objects.
- Less effective with larger objects that are harder to hide.
- Masking the issue doesn't remove it fully.

{{< /tab >}}
{{< tab "Increase Complexity (risky)" >}}

Sometimes, adding *more* detail can fix an evenness issue. For instance, subdividing an object (and making sure the division isn't even) can help fix some evenness issues.

However as mentioned before, evenness can also get worse the more complex your deco is. That's what makes this method so risky; it can be like spamming objects on your deco to try and make it look "better", but only making it look worse.

**Pros:**
- Relatively easy, especially when starting to decorate an object.
- Most people won't notice unless they're trained to spot evenness.

**Cons:**
- Insanely risky; can backfire and introduce more evenness.
- Time-consuming as adding more detail takes a lot of time and resources.

## Ignoring Evenness

There are a few instances where you should actually *ignore* evenness in your work, or introduce it intentionally. It takes experience and skill to know when to ignore it, but some scenarios are listed below.

### Exception 1: Repetition

While it's important to have dynamic deco, having "too much" can become way too chaotic. **Intentionally duplicating and reusing a detail in your deco** can make it feel more unified.

For instance, if you have thirty different types of spikes in your level, it becomes much harder to process since many different pieces of deco serve the same function. It's more effective to use only two or three spike designs in a part, so there isn't as much to process.

The image below does just this. The spikes and saws are reused through the part. Additionally, the design in the top left features some clear repetition. Not only does this save time (since I can just use {{< img src="images/GDEmotes/Buttons/Copy+Paste.png" class="emote">}}), but it also prevents the deco from getting too chaotic.

{{< img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1W1Ee3u4pvLvGVRkB9EcSna_moi2Zqw_p">}}

### Exception 2: Echoing

Echoing is similar to repetition, but not exactly the same. While repetition is repeating the *exact same* element or detail, echoing is **repeating the same idea** in your work. Just like repetition, this helps make your deco more unified, though it is more advanced as it allows room for more variety and complexity.

An example of echoing is this art by Sin jong hun. The ruins, buildings, and even the environment reuse the idea of concentric circles. While this idea appears in very different contexts (such as manmade structures and nature), the general idea is reused multiple times.

{{< img src="https://cdna.artstation.com/p/assets/images/images/018/773/854/large/shin-jong-hun-asdasf.jpg?1560684630">}}

Here's a breakdown of some echoing in this art.

### Exception 3: Directionality

Directionality is basically **how objects "point" in a direction**.

For instance, a circle doesn't "point" in a direction because it doesn't have any points. However, a triangle (e.g., an arrow symbol ➡️) shows direction.

While this is *incredibly* useful for shape design and composition, as you'll see in later guides, it's also important to have some "rest areas" where your eyes aren't constantly being moved around. This way, you aren't constantly overwhelming viewers.

Here's another example by Sin jong hun. While there are many shapes that convey direction in this art, there are also many rest areas.

### Exception 4: Rhythm

Rhythm is similar to directionality, but more general; you **lead the viewer's eye around** by creating *patterns* in your work. Intuitively, this requires some sort of repetition or echoing.

Here's an example by Zhu Weiwei. The rocks in the foreground and background are arranged to lead your eye through the scene. They all share similar shapes, lighting, color, and texture, which helps make this more effective.

### Exception 5: Symmetry

Symmetry is likely the most intuitive of these concepts – you **directly mirror an object along one or many axes**. There are many types of symmetry (e.g., reflecting objects across lines vs. rotating them around points), but they all involve some sort of repetition.

Importantly, when used right symmetry can be a very powerful tool. For instance, a manmade building with clear symmetry can stand out against an environment with no such symmetry.

Symmetry can be seen frequently with objects such as entrances and doorways, but a specific art example is the DOOM Hunter Base from Doom Eternal. Its clear symmetry helps it stand out against the asymmetry in the background.

# 3: Advanced Theory

Let's say you're dividing an object into two parts. As we've discussed, you'll likely want to avoid a perfect 50:50 split (or ratio) between the parts. But **is there a “best” ratio to divide your object with?** This question is one I'll "answer" here.

In reality, there is no "best" ratio as it depends on what you want. Obviously, a 50:50 ratio is even, meaning both parts will look the same. However, depending on the circumstances you may not want a ratio like 90:10 as it makes one object *far* too small.

However, there is a decent ratio you can use in a variety of circumstances: **70:30**.

## 70:30 Rule

This ratio is incredibly effective for many reasons:

- While both sections have clear hierarchy (one object is definitely larger, compared to a ratio like 51:49), neither overpowers the other like a ratio like 90:10 can risk.

{{< img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/14H5zwNS0XPDSXSyUBvzL75__T21eBwm_">}}

- 7 and 3 are both prime numbers, which makes it harder to get evenness by dividing each section into smaller sections.

{{< img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1FkS4_d8OKMRIsre0C3L7oBJhzSDRR9jd">}}

- It can be generalized very nicely, as you'll see in the next section.

## Generalizing 70:30

How do you generalize the 70/30 rule to more sections? Well, the answer is math!

After dividing an object into two sections, you can add a third section to it. Make sure this third section follows the 70/30 rule with respect to the second section (so you have another 70/30 ratio).

By doing so, you get another commonly used ratio: 62:27:11, or the commonly described **60:30:10** rule. This is more applicable to complex shapes and color distribution, since you're more likely to use three colors, values, or shapes together than you are to just use two.

More generally, this "optimal ratio" is calculated with the following formula:

$$
S(i, n) = \frac{100 * (7/3)^i}{\sum_{i=0}^{n} (7/3)^i}
$$

This essentially means that you start with the number 1, multiply it by $(7/3)^n$ times, sum the results, and then divide each individual number by that sum.

Since this is annoying to do for every individual set of numbers, I created an [evenness calculator](https://www.desmos.com/calculator/whhtaju8xb) you can use to see these theoretical "best values" for yourself.

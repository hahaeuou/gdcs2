# 1: What are Guides?

Guides are informative articles for all Geometry Dash creators to use. Each guide focuses on a **particular skill** creators should learn, like setting up a certain trigger or how to approach making block designs. Guides are primarily posted within the GDCS website, but they may also be adapted into YouTube videos.

# 2: How Do I Write a Guide?

The writing process for guides is marked by certain **milestones**. You’ll start from a template explaining the basics of your guide’s topic, build out a list of topics, flesh out this list into words, and create examples to help explain your words. This gives more structure than just throwing you into a fire and asking you to figure it out.

At each milestone, **you can get feedback from other contributors**. You can also **ask other creators for help** with this process, so you’re never alone when writing guides.

Here’s a brief explanation of each milestone we look for.

## Basic guide summary

When starting a guide, you may use the following document with a [pre-filled template](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1BzUtVuLmERhh1jwAmpY3fKim-tUwGuLIx8HVZYQcjas/edit?usp=sharing). This template will contain a basic structure of the guide’s content.

Start by **doing any research you need to understand the topic**, like asking knowledgeable people, reading related sources, or experimenting with it yourself. Then, make a short **bullet-point summary** of the guide’s topics. Restating these topics in your own words should help you understand them, and it also serves as a quick reference when you build out the rest of the guide.

Here’s an example summary for a guide on [Low Detail Mode](/docs/guides/deco-1/low-detail-mode/).

{{< callout context="note" title="TLDR - What this guide covers" icon="outline/info-circle" >}}

- Levels tend to lag due to high object densities and complex object properties, especially with trigger usage.
- Tick “High Detail” to an object to toggle it off when Low Detail Mode is on or use toggle triggers if you need more customization.
- Optimization refers to reducing the amount of objects used without affecting its appearance, which is important for creating less lag when playing levels.
- Some important efficiency skills to include are overlapping objects, using scaling (scalehack and/or warping), and using certain object archetypes to achieve something in a sustainable manner.
- Reducing objects can be done by creating a second copy of any group of objects that need to be optimized and using efficiency skills to reduce object count.

## Full topic list

Your guide template also has space for the guide’s topic list. Here, you’ll **list the topics your guide will cover**. For instance, a guide on the Move trigger could have three topics: one on its basic XY mode, a section on Target & Direction mode, and a third which covers Easings. The topic list also informs the sections and subsections in the guide.

**This milestone is *not required***. If you’re working on a simple guide, it’s totally fine to jump straight to writing. **However, we strongly encourage it**, as it’ll help with organization and ensure you don’t misjudge how large your guide will be.

## Topics written out

In this step, you’ll actually write your guide. Your guide template has space for each topic, which you can use to write your guide.

Writing can understandably be difficult – it’s the longest part of making a guide. However, **you can do a *lot* to speed this up**. We recommend doing the following:

- **Plan your visuals** – images, videos, and diagrams can often explain more than words and imagination. When writing, you can add placeholder text to indicate what visuals you’ll add.
- **Leverage your sources** – it’s often easier to quote another person’s writing than to reinvent the wheel. If your guide has sources, feel free to draw direct quotes from them while giving proper credit.
- **Use the Minto Pyramid** – state the biggest takeaway or most important information first (this is usually what the concept is), explain why they’re important, then elaborate on the details last (when, where, and how you can apply the concept). This document is intended to be written using this pyramid, for example.

Instead of agonizing over how you explain a tough topic, these methods should help you get words on paper first. You should also ask for feedback to make sure your points make sense. You can get feedback from anyone, even people outside the guide team.

## Examples created

Finally, you’ll **supplement your writing with visual examples**. You aren’t required to make your own examples (we have people dedicated to this) but it’s helpful to think through what examples you want either way.

We recommend doing the following to speed up exercise creation:

- **Plan your formats** – think through what each is trying to say, and the best way to say it. For instance, videos are better at explaining animations than images. You can discuss best practices with other guide makers.
- **Leverage text in examples** – small captions for images, text in videos, and short explanations can help enhance your examples. You can also ask for help editing examples as well.
- **Stay organized** – keep your examples in one place so they can easily be referenced or iterated on. When starting a guide, we’ll provide you with a thread in the guide-progress channel. Feel free to dump examples in your thread.

## Sources

Normally, guide sources will be links to sites or the names of books. The format for naming a source is always the name of the website/author (whichever exists), then the title of the source. An example is “[Mental Checkpoint: The Last Game Dev Advice You Need](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L-zKm6c8_IE)”. Always display the guide sources using a bulleted list.

If a source is a paid textbook or other paid content, state the name and author, as well as what sections were referenced. Do not paste any links to the content if they are available in the directory.

If the source says “The Game”, simply remove this section. Announce to everyone that you have lost, too.

# 3: What help can I get?

Writing is *not* easy. There are many involved steps to the writing process, and you must understand the topic before you can begin. However, that doesn’t mean it must be done alone. As a contributor, you have many resources available for use to make guide writing easier, more collaborative, and more fun.

## Guide Collabs
This is the most straightforward option: **you can work with others on your guide**. For instance, one person could write the first section of a guide, while the other person works on a second part. Alternatively, one person could do the writing while another works on examples.

There are many ways to approach guide collabs, so don’t feel restricted or deterred from it. We suggest working with people you already know, or with other contributors. If you can’t find anyone to work with, notify a *Head Contributor* on the discord server and **we can get someone to help you**.

## Example Support
If you’d like high-quality examples but don’t have the time to make them, you can ask anyone on the discord server with the *Guide Examples* role for help. Just say the example format you want, what the example should depict, and when you need it completed by.

It helps to keep example discussion in the same thread as your guide document. This way, others can understand the context around the example and what to include when making it.

## Feedback Sessions
If you’re struggling with your guide’s content, you can **set up a feedback session with a *Head Contributor***. This will be a conversation (through text or voice chat) where you let us know where you need help and get advice in turn. We can also help with brainstorming, phrasing content, and any concerns that arise during the guide process.
